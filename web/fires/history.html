U.S. Warehouse Fire History: Baseline Statistics

Key Finding

The April 7, 2026 Kimberly-Clark fire caused an estimated $500–650 million in damage. That single fire equals or exceeds the total annual warehouse fire losses for the entire United States in any recent year on record ($283M–$323M average).

Long-Term Trend (1980–2022)

U.S. warehouse fires have declined dramatically over four decades, driven primarily by widespread adoption of automatic sprinkler systems and improved building codes — a ~73% decline from the 1980 peak.

On the 15% vs. 7% discrepancy: These figures come from two different NFPA reports using different methodologies. The 2016–2020 figure of "15% arson" likely includes fires classified as suspicious, undetermined, or under investigation — a broader category. The 2018–2022 figure of "7% intentional" reflects only fires confirmed as intentionally set. Neither is wrong; they measure different things.

Prior Labor-Motivated Arson (for Comparison)

Research found no prior nationally covered U.S. case of a warehouse worker explicitly citing a "living wage" as the stated motive for arson before April 7, 2026. Adjacent historical patterns:

  • Eco-terrorism (ELF/ALF, 1990s–2000s): Documented ideological arson of construction sites, auto dealerships, and research facilities with stated motives; tracked by FBI as domestic terrorism. The closest structural analog to the Ontario case.
  • Rage/termination fires: Isolated cases of fired employees targeting workplaces (e.g., the March 16, 2026 Flushing, Queens case — Roman Amatitla). Employment-related but not wage-protest framing.
  • Historical labor sabotage: Pre-NLRA era (early 20th century) included documented workplace sabotage connected to labor organizing. Not a significant documented pattern in post-WWII U.S. fire records.

The Ontario case is the first in the current dataset with an on-camera, explicit wage-protest statement.

Data Gaps and Limitations

  • No annual warehouse-specific arson count by year (2015–2025) was found in publicly available NFIRS summaries. NFPA reports aggregate multi-year averages.
  • 2023 and 2024 warehouse fire counts are not yet available in published NFPA reports as of April 2026.
  • The 2021–2022 uptick (from ~1,339 to ~1,685 fires per year) is noted in the NFPA February 2026 report but not fully explained. Possible factors: post-COVID supply chain expansion, increased EV/battery fires, aging logistics infrastructure.
  • The April 2026 cluster is too recent to appear in any official dataset. NFIRS data typically lags 12–18 months.
